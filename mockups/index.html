Why the Recommended layout, specifically

  • Story sits above the sell. The reason people open a Talking Hens email is Jason's voice — so the letter leads, and commerce sits below his signature. That ordering is the soft-sell, built into the page.
  • The highest-margin product gets the one loud button. Live birds are the money. "In the yard this week" is the visual climax — a green card with the single boldest CTA ("See this week's birds"). In Variant A the hens were an unlinked list; here they're the primary action.
  • One primary action per zone, no click-scatter. A quiet outline link for the blog (engagement), a loud button for the birds (revenue), a low-key product row for "while you're here." No competing buttons fighting above the fold like Variant C.
  • Built to survive a real inbox. Headline is real HTML text below the image (never overlaid — C's headline & CTA would vanish with images off). No drop cap, web-safe serif (Georgia) with optional Fraunces, CSS-dot bullets instead of emoji, single-column that stacks cleanly on mobile and in Outlook.
  • Brand continuity kept. Same logo, green/cream palette, hero, Hens block and Merricks footer as today — only the arrangement changes, so it still reads as them.

Notes for production (Klaviyo): these are design mockups — the real build is laid into Jason's Klaviyo template using table-based, bulletproof buttons and real <img> heroes/products with alt text (the gradients here are placeholders). One nice first-send touch the brand lens suggested: a single Jason-voiced line under the masthead — "We've slimmed these down so it's more of a letter and less of a flyer — same girls, same yard." — so the format change reads as intention, not error. Recommend phasing it in over a send or two (bike method) and watching open/click before retiring the old template.
